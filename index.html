5 active drug corners in Philly

By Joe Brandt | 9 March 2016

Enterprising journalists in the city have focused on poverty and unemployment as factors spurring a rise in the city's drug trade.

"They are selling drugs in the shadows of closed-down factories that used to employ their parents and grandparents," Penn anthropologist Philippe Bourgois told the Inquirer in 2010. "You'd almost have to be abnormal not to go into the drug trade."

Across the city, certain destinations are studies in poverty. Arrest statistics show where people are most commonly cited for drug possession or for dealing.

Most of those locations are in Kensington or Fairhill, or the area between (colloquially called "The Badlands.") Steve Volk, a journalist who compiled a list of prominent city drug corners for Phawker and Philadelphia Weekly, stressed that work was being done to remove drugs from these communities.

Still, Volk writes, it was not uncommon to see teenagers "slinging" drugs, most commonly heroin but also crack and PCP-coated cigarettes, with young children acting as lookouts for patroling police.

"And like all illicit economies, the drug trade begets a brutal gangsterism whose stock in trade is violence — violence on an industrial scale. The statistics are as astonishing as they are appalling. 'We’ve had 16,000 shootings here in the last 10 years,'' says Assistant U.S. Attorney Rob Reed. “Sixteen thousand!” That averages out to four Philadelphians being shot every day, or one citizen every six hours. Since 2008, more Americans have been murdered in Philadelphia than killed in Iraq."
